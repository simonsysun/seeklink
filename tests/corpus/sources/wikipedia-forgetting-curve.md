# Forgetting curve (Wikipedia summary)

*Summary paraphrased from the Wikipedia article.*

The forgetting curve describes the decrease in retention of information
over time when there is no attempt at retention. First characterized by
**Hermann Ebbinghaus** in 1885 in his monograph *Memory: A Contribution
to Experimental Psychology*, the curve remains a central reference for
memory research and learning system design.

## Shape

Classical reports of the curve show very rapid initial decline —
roughly half of freshly learned material is forgotten within the first
hour, and about two-thirds within 24 hours for meaningless material
with no rehearsal. The remaining fraction decays much more slowly, and
a residue persists for days to weeks.

Modern empirical studies on larger populations and with meaningful
material (as opposed to Ebbinghaus's nonsense syllables) find the same
qualitative shape but better fit with a **power law** than Ebbinghaus's
originally proposed exponential form.

## Mathematical models

- **Ebbinghaus (1885)**: proposed an exponential-decay-like form with
  decreasing marginal rate.
- **Wickelgren (1972)**: power-function memory trace model.
- **Wixted & Ebbesen (1991)**: empirical demonstration that power laws
  fit better than exponentials across many tasks.
- **DSR (Difficulty-Stability-Retrievability) framework (Wozniak et
  al., 1990s)**: three-parameter model underlying SuperMemo algorithms.

The FSRS algorithm, developed in 2022-2023 by Jarrett Ye and
collaborators, is a modern version of the DSR framework with 17 learned
parameters fitted on population review data.

## Countermeasures

The forgetting curve can be flattened by:

1. **Active recall** — retrieving the memory, not re-reading. Each
   retrieval attempt strengthens the trace disproportionately.
2. **Spacing** — distributing retrieval over time, as opposed to
   cramming. The spacing effect is one of the most robust findings in
   cognitive psychology.
3. **Sleep** — consolidation during sleep transfers memory from
   hippocampus to cortex and slows decay.
4. **Meaning and depth of processing** — material encoded with
   elaboration (linking to prior knowledge, generating examples)
   decays more slowly than rote material.

## Implications for learners

Without systematic review, most of any study session is forgotten
within a month. With a review pattern tuned to catch each item near
the retrievability threshold (typically 90%), retention at one year can
exceed 85%. This is the quantitative case for spaced repetition systems.
