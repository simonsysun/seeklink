# Spaced repetition (Wikipedia summary)

*Summary of the Wikipedia article, paraphrased for this test corpus.*

Spaced repetition is a learning technique in which newly introduced
material is reviewed at gradually increasing intervals. The effect is
consistent across subject matter, age groups, and cultures, and has
been validated in meta-analyses spanning over a century of research.

## History

The phenomenon was first characterized by **Hermann Ebbinghaus** in
the 1880s through experiments on himself using nonsense syllables. He
documented the forgetting curve — retention drops sharply in the first
24 hours and stabilizes at a low plateau thereafter if no review
intervenes. Subsequent researchers extended his work to meaningful
material and demonstrated the **spacing effect**: distributed practice
(multiple short sessions spread over time) outperforms massed practice
(one long session) for the same total time.

## Modern implementations

The first computer-based spaced repetition system was **SuperMemo**,
developed by Piotr Wozniak starting in 1985. SuperMemo introduced the
SM-2 algorithm, which is still widely used in flashcard applications.
Later systems include Anki, Mnemosyne, and Memrise.

More recent algorithms — notably **FSRS (Free Spaced Repetition
Scheduler)** developed by Jarrett Ye — replace SM-2's fixed formula
with a data-driven model fitted on millions of real review logs. FSRS
explicitly models retrievability, stability, and difficulty as
parameters of a power-law forgetting curve.

## Applications

Medical education (pharmacology, anatomy), foreign language
acquisition (vocabulary), bar exam preparation, and programming
certification all report significant improvements when switching from
re-reading to spaced repetition. The effect size is typically in the
range of 1.5-3× retention at equal time investment.

## Limitations

Spaced repetition excels at atomic facts (vocabulary, formulas, named
entities) but does not automatically produce *deep* understanding. It
complements but does not replace study techniques aimed at
comprehension (such as the Feynman technique or concept mapping).
