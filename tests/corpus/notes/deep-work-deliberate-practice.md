# Deep work and deliberate practice

Two overlapping concepts that are often conflated:

**Deep work** (Cal Newport) — professional activity performed in a
state of distraction-free concentration that pushes your cognitive
capabilities to their limit. Opposite: shallow work (email, meetings,
context-switching).

**Deliberate practice** (Anders Ericsson) — structured, high-effort
practice with immediate feedback, targeting specifically your edge
of competence. Opposite: mindless repetition of what you already can do.

The overlap: both demand sustained concentrated attention on tasks just
above your current level. The difference: deep work is a *state*,
deliberate practice is a *method*. You can do deep work without
deliberately practicing (e.g. writing a report you already know how
to write). You can attempt deliberate practice without deep work
(e.g. practicing piano with TV on — useless).

## Why this matters for learning systems

Spaced repetition, Zettelkasten, and Feynman technique all assume
deliberate practice conditions. If you're reviewing Anki cards while
watching YouTube, you're not learning, you're moving pixels. The
tooling doesn't save you.

Typical signs of sham practice:
- You can't remember what you reviewed 30 minutes ago
- You rate everything "good" to get through the queue
- You read the same chapter three times without taking notes

## The daily structure that works

For a knowledge worker: **2-4 hours of deep work, early in the day,
before meetings/email/messaging.** Protect it ritualistically. This
is where learning, writing, and real thinking happen. Everything else
fills the shallow afternoon.
